Ethereum (ETH)

✅ Verified 📅 Last updated: 2024-01 📂 Cryptocurrencies
📋 Contents

Ethereum (ETH) didn't just add to the crypto revolution — it turbo-charged it. When Vitalik Buterin, a 19-year-old Russian-Canadian programmer, looked at Bitcoin and asked "what if the blockchain could run any program?" — he planted the seed of an entire digital civilisation. Smart contracts, DeFi, NFTs, DAOs, stablecoins, Web3 — it all flows from that one idea: a programmable, trustless, global computer. Ethereum is the world's settlement layer for decentralised finance, and it's just getting started.

History & Origins

Vitalik's Vision (2013)

Vitalik Buterin was born on 31 January 1994 in Moscow, raised in Canada. A Bitcoin enthusiast from the age of 17, he co-founded Bitcoin Magazine in 2011 before growing frustrated with Bitcoin's intentional limitations as a scripting platform. In November 2013, at just 19 years old, he published the Ethereum whitepaper — proposing a general-purpose blockchain with a built-in Turing-complete programming language. Bitcoin was a calculator. Ethereum would be a smartphone.

The co-founders who joined Vitalik to build the vision included Gavin Wood (who wrote the Yellow Paper and created the Solidity language), Joseph Lubin (later founder of ConsenSys), Charles Hoskinson (later founder of Cardano), Anthony Di Iorio, and Jeffrey Wilcke.

The $18.4M Crowdsale (2014)

In the summer of 2014, the Ethereum Foundation ran a public crowdsale — one of the earliest legitimate token sales in crypto history. Over 42 days, they raised $18.4 million in Bitcoin, selling ETH at a price of approximately 2,000 ETH per BTC (roughly $0.30 per ETH at the time). The sale provided the funding to build out the protocol. Those early buyers sitting on their ETH through 2021 saw a return of over 15,000x at the ATH.

Mainnet Launch: Frontier (2015)

The Ethereum mainnet launched on 30 July 2015, in the "Frontier" phase — deliberately bare-bones and targeted at developers. It was raw, buggy, and beautiful. Within months, hundreds of developers were writing Solidity contracts and deploying the first dApps. The ERC-20 token standard, which would power the entire ICO era, was formalised in 2015–2016.

The DAO Hack & the Great Fork (2016)

In June 2016, a vulnerability in "The DAO" — a decentralised investment fund holding $150M worth of ETH (3.6 million ETH) — was exploited by an attacker who drained approximately $60 million. This triggered the most controversial debate in Ethereum's history: should the community perform a hard fork to reverse the theft, or respect code-is-law absolutism?

The community voted to fork — rolling back the chain and returning funds to investors. The minority who refused to follow created Ethereum Classic (ETC), preserving the original chain. The fork was deeply divisive but pragmatic: Ethereum chose the welfare of its users over philosophical purity. Today, ETC remains a minor chain while Ethereum has gone on to $500B+ valuations.

The ICO Boom & CryptoKitties (2017)

The ERC-20 token standard transformed Ethereum into a fundraising machine. In 2017, projects raised billions through Initial Coin Offerings (ICOs), issuing tokens on Ethereum. Some were legitimate; many were scams. The ICO boom drove ETH from ~$8 at the start of 2017 to $1,432 in January 2018.

In December 2017, a blockchain game called CryptoKitties went viral — users were breeding and trading digital cats for up to $100,000 each. The game clogged the Ethereum network, spiking gas fees and demonstrating both the demand for and scalability challenges of the platform. CryptoKitties was the first viral NFT moment.

DeFi Summer (2020)

Summer 2020 was when Ethereum's smart contract thesis was validated spectacularly. Compound launched liquidity mining, paying users COMP tokens to borrow and lend. Uniswap v2 enabled anyone to create liquidity pools and trade any ERC-20 token without an order book. Yearn Finance automated yield optimisation. Total Value Locked (TVL) in DeFi protocols exploded from under $1B to over $10B in months. This was the moment it became undeniable: Ethereum was the backbone of a new financial system. See also Tokenomics for how token incentives drove DeFi growth.

EIP-1559: The Fee Burn (2021)

The London upgrade on 5 August 2021 implemented EIP-1559, fundamentally changing Ethereum's fee market. Instead of paying all fees to miners as pure profit, a base fee is permanently burned — removed from circulation — with only the "tip" going to miners. This transformed ETH from a pure inflationary asset into one that could become deflationary during periods of high network activity. As of 2024, over 4.4 million ETH have been burned via EIP-1559 — billions of dollars in supply permanently removed.

ATH $4,891 (2021)

The combined effects of DeFi, NFTs exploding (OpenSea, Bored Apes), institutional interest, and EIP-1559's supply squeeze drove ETH to an all-time high of $4,891 on 10 November 2021 — the same day Bitcoin hit its $69,044 ATH. The Ethereum ecosystem's market cap briefly touched $580 billion.

The Merge: PoW → PoS (2022)

On 15 September 2022, Ethereum completed one of the most technically complex upgrades in the history of software: The Merge. The existing proof-of-work execution layer was merged with the Beacon Chain's proof-of-stake consensus layer, transitioning Ethereum from energy-intensive mining to staking virtually overnight. The result: a 99.95% reduction in energy consumption. ETH issuance dropped by ~90%. The "triple halving" narrative was born — the Merge was equivalent to three Bitcoin halvings happening simultaneously in terms of supply impact.

Shanghai/Capella: Unstaking (2023)

The Shanghai/Capella upgrade on 12 April 2023 enabled withdrawal of staked ETH for the first time — a feature validators had been waiting for since the Beacon Chain launched in December 2020. Contrary to fears of a mass exodus, the upgrade was orderly and actually increased staking participation, as the new ability to unstake removed the primary barrier to entry for new stakers.

Dencun & EIP-4844: L2 Revolution (2024)

The Dencun upgrade on 13 March 2024 implemented EIP-4844 (proto-danksharding), introducing "blobs" — a new data storage format specifically designed for Layer 2 rollups. The effect was immediate and dramatic: L2 transaction fees dropped by 90–99% overnight. Arbitrum, Optimism, Base, and zkSync transactions that once cost $0.10–$1.00 dropped to fractions of a cent. Ethereum's L2 ecosystem had its rocket fuel.

Spot ETH ETF (2024)

Following Bitcoin's ETF approval, the SEC approved spot Ethereum ETFs on 23 May 2024, with trading beginning on 23 July 2024. Products from BlackRock, Fidelity, and others brought institutional-grade Ethereum exposure to traditional investors, further cementing ETH's place in the mainstream financial landscape.

How It Works

The Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM)

The Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM) is the runtime environment for smart contracts. It is a Turing-complete, sandboxed computing environment that runs identically on every node in the network. When you deploy a smart contract (written in Solidity or Vyper), it gets compiled to EVM bytecode and executed deterministically by every validator. This is what makes Ethereum trustless: the code executes exactly as written, with no possibility of server downtime, censorship, or manipulation.

Gas Fees & EIP-1559

Every operation on the EVM costs gas — a measure of computational work. Gas is paid in ETH. Under EIP-1559, fees consist of a base fee (burned, determined algorithmically by network demand) plus an optional priority tip (paid to validators). When the network is congested, the base fee rises; when quiet, it falls. This creates predictable fee pricing and, crucially, the burning mechanism that makes ETH deflationary at high usage levels. Learn more about this in Tokenomics.

Proof of Stake & Staking

Post-Merge, Ethereum's security comes from validators who stake a minimum of 32 ETH as collateral. Validators are randomly selected to propose and attest to blocks. They earn staking rewards (~4–5% APY) for honest participation and face slashing (loss of staked ETH) for malicious behaviour. As of 2025, over 34 million ETH (28%+ of supply) is staked. You can also stake via wallets through liquid staking protocols like Lido or Rocket Pool with no 32 ETH minimum.

Layer 2 Scaling

Ethereum's base layer intentionally limits throughput to maximise decentralisation. Layer 2 rollups handle scale by executing transactions off-chain and posting compressed proofs or data back to L1. Leading L2s include:

  • Arbitrum — Optimistic rollup, largest L2 by TVL
  • Optimism / OP Stack — Optimistic rollup powering Base, Mode, Zora
  • Base — Coinbase's L2 built on OP Stack, massive retail adoption
  • zkSync & StarkNet — ZK-rollups using zero-knowledge proofs for instant finality

Post-Dencun, L2 fees are below $0.01 for most transactions — making Ethereum-secured finance genuinely accessible to everyone.

Why It Matters

Ethereum is the infrastructure layer of the decentralised internet. Here's what it powers:

  • DeFi: Lending, borrowing, trading, yield farming — trillions in value have flowed through Ethereum-based DeFi protocols. Uniswap alone has processed over $2 trillion in trading volume.
  • NFTs: The global market for NFTs was built on Ethereum's ERC-721 and ERC-1155 standards. From Bored Apes to digital art auctions, Ethereum remains the premium NFT settlement layer.
  • Web3: Web3 — the vision of user-owned internet — is built predominantly on Ethereum. DAOs, on-chain governance, decentralised social media, and DID (decentralised identity) all flow from the EVM ecosystem.
  • Stablecoins: USDT, USDC, and DAI — the major dollar-pegged stablecoins — run primarily on Ethereum. Over $150B in stablecoin value uses Ethereum as its settlement layer, making it the backbone of global crypto commerce.
  • Developer Activity: Ethereum has more active developers than any other blockchain, consistently. The EVM has been adopted by hundreds of chains (BNB Chain, Polygon, Avalanche, etc.), cementing Solidity as the dominant smart contract language.

Risks & Controversies

  • Lido Dominance: Lido Finance controls approximately ~32% of all staked ETH (as of early 2025; subject to change — check current data) — a single liquid staking protocol with that much stake concentration raises centralisation concerns. If Lido validators acted maliciously, they could affect consensus. The community actively debates whether this is an existential risk.
  • SEC Classification: The SEC has debated whether ETH is a security. The approval of spot ETH ETFs in 2024 largely resolved this in the US, but regulatory uncertainty in other jurisdictions remains.
  • Complexity & Attack Surface: Ethereum's programmability is its greatest strength and its greatest risk. Smart contract bugs have led to billions in losses. The more complex the code, the larger the attack surface.
  • L2 Fragmentation: The proliferation of Layer 2 chains creates a fragmented user experience. Liquidity is split across dozens of rollups, bridges are complex and occasionally exploited, and the average user finds the L2 landscape confusing.
  • Competition: Exchanges and investors increasingly route activity to faster/cheaper chains like Solana. Ethereum must continue delivering on its roadmap to maintain developer and user loyalty.
