NFTs (Non-Fungible Tokens)

✅ Verified📅 Last updated: 2024-01📂 Concepts
📋 Contents

NFTs made artists millionaires, turned JPEGs into $69 million assets, and then crashed 95%. But underneath the hype, NFTs solved something genuinely revolutionary: provable digital ownership on the blockchain. From CryptoPunks to Bored Apes to digital music royalties to in-game items — NFTs are the ownership layer of the internet. The mania may have cooled, but the technology didn't go anywhere.

History & Origins

Precursors (2012–2016)

The concept of unique digital assets on the blockchain predates Ethereum. Colored Coins (2012) on Bitcoin attempted to represent real-world assets. The Counterparty platform (2014), also built on Bitcoin, enabled early NFT-like assets — most notably Rare Pepes from 2016, pixelated frog trading cards that attracted a genuine collector community and prices in the thousands of dollars.

Birth of Modern NFTs (2017)

2017 changed everything. CryptoPunks launched June 23, 2017, created by Larva Labs (Matt Hall and John Watkinson) — 10,000 algorithmically generated 24×24 pixel art characters, originally claimable for free by anyone with an Ethereum wallet. Nobody cared at first.

Then in November 2017, CryptoKitties launched and literally congested the Ethereum network — people were spending $172,000 on a digital cat. The world noticed. In January 2018, the ERC-721 standard was formally proposed by William Entriken, Dieter Shirley, and others, giving NFTs a universal technical blueprint.

The Great NFT Boom (2020–2022)

NBA Top Shot brought sports collectibles on-chain in 2020, onboarding millions of mainstream users. Then 2021 happened:

  • March 11, 2021 — Beeple's "Everydays: The First 5000 Days" sold at Christie's for $69.3 million, the third-highest price ever achieved by a living artist at auction. The world lost its mind.
  • April 2021 — Bored Ape Yacht Club (BAYC) launched by Yuga Labs at 0.08 ETH each. Floor price eventually hit 150 ETH (~$440,000). Celebrities from Eminem to Steph Curry flexed their Apes as profile pictures.
  • June 2021 — CryptoPunk #7523 sold for $11.75 million. Those "worthless" free Punks were now trophy assets.
  • August 2021 — OpenSea did $3.4 billion in volume in a single month.
  • Total 2021 NFT market volume: $25 billion.

The Crash & Aftermath (2022–2024)

The bear market was brutal. NFT floor prices dropped 90%+ across the board. Yuga Labs acquired CryptoPunks and Meebits in January 2022, consolidating the blue-chip NFT ecosystem. Blur launched in October 2022 with a pro-trader focus and airdrop rewards, overtaking OpenSea in volume. The royalty wars (2022–2023) saw marketplaces making creator royalties optional, devastating artist revenue. Bitcoin Ordinals launched in January 2023 via Casey Rodarmor, inscribing 60M+ NFTs directly on Bitcoin. Trump NFT trading cards (Dec 2022) sold for $9.5M in hours — proving that demand for digital collectibles never fully died.

By 2024–2025, the narrative shifted toward NFT gaming items, music NFTs (Royal, Sound.xyz), and real-world asset (RWA) tokenisation — NFTs as infrastructure, not just speculation.

How They Work / Key Concepts

Token Standards: ERC-721 vs ERC-1155

ERC-721 is the original NFT standard — each token ID is unique and non-interchangeable. ERC-1155 is the multi-token standard, supporting both fungible and non-fungible tokens in a single contract — ideal for gaming where you might have 1,000 identical swords and 1 unique legendary weapon.

Minting & Metadata

"Minting" creates an NFT by executing a smart contract function that assigns a unique token ID to an address. The NFT's metadata (name, image, attributes) is stored either on-chain (expensive but permanent) or via IPFS (decentralised, more common) or centralised servers (risky — the company can change or delete your JPEG). A crypto wallet is required to hold and transfer NFTs.

Marketplaces

OpenSea remains the largest NFT marketplace by cumulative volume ($34B+). Blur dominates active pro-trader volume with its points-and-airdrop model. Magic Eden rules the Solana NFT ecosystem. Each marketplace charges fees and historically enforced (or not enforced) creator royalties differently — a source of major controversy.

Provenance & Ownership

The blockchain is the ledger of record. Every transfer of an NFT is permanently recorded on-chain — you can trace a CryptoPunk's ownership history back to 2017. This immutable provenance is what gives blue-chip NFTs their perceived value. Unlike traditional art, forgery is mathematically impossible on-chain.

Why It Matters

NFTs solve a fundamental problem of the digital world: you can copy a JPEG infinitely, but you can't copy blockchain ownership. The implications cascade:

  • Creator royalties — Artists earn every time their work resells, programmable in the smart contract
  • Gaming asset ownership — Your in-game sword is yours, not the game company's
  • Digital identity — PFP (profile picture) culture as online identity, token-gated communities
  • Ticketing & access — NFT tickets that can't be counterfeited
  • Real-world asset tokenisation — Fractional ownership of real estate, art, and luxury goods

The Web3 vision of user-owned internet requires an ownership primitive. NFTs, built on Ethereum and verified by blockchain technology, are that primitive.

Risks & Controversies

⚠️
High Risk: NFT markets are highly speculative, illiquid during downturns, and subject to manipulation, theft, and platform risk. DYOR always.
  • Wash trading — Inflated volume figures from self-dealing. OpenSea and Blur volumes should be taken with scepticism.
  • Stolen NFTs — Phishing attacks and approvals exploits have drained wallets of six-figure assets overnight.
  • Centralised metadata — If your NFT's image is hosted on a company's server (not IPFS), it can disappear. "Right-click save as" becomes literal.
  • Bear market wipeout — 90–95% price drops from peak. Many 2021 minters are still deep in the red.
  • Copyright confusion — Owning an NFT does not automatically convey IP rights to the underlying art. BAYC granted commercial rights; most collections don't.
  • The FOMO Plague drove many to mint at peak prices. The NGMI Chronicles are full of those stories.

The Crypto Moonboys Take 🌙

"We didn't buy JPEGs. We bought culture, community, and blockchain-verified flex. BAYC is the Rolling Stones of crypto — the floor might be lower, but the legend is permanent."

The 2021 Moon Mission that Apes went on was unlike anything in crypto history. Free mints becoming $440k assets. Discord communities becoming exclusive social clubs. Celebrities paying hundreds of ETH for cartoon pictures. Was it rational? Debatable. Was it fun? Absolutely.

The FOMO during mint season was real and dangerous. Plenty of people minted 10 NFTs at 1 ETH each and are still holding the bags. But the ones who understood the technology — who saw NFTs as the ownership layer of the next internet — those are the diamond hands who'll be vindicated when digital ownership is as normal as a deed to a house.
