NFTs (Non-Fungible Tokens)

✅ Verified📅 Last updated: 2024-01📂 Concepts
📋 Contents

Non-Fungible Tokens (NFTs) are unique digital assets stored on a blockchain that represent ownership of a one-of-a-kind item — whether digital art, music, collectibles, in-game items, real estate deeds, or anything else. Unlike fungible tokens (e.g., BTC or ETH, where one unit is identical to another), each NFT has a unique identifier making it irreplaceable.

Overview

NFTs use blockchain technology to prove provenance and ownership. When someone "owns" an NFT, the blockchain serves as an immutable public record of that ownership. Most NFTs exist on Ethereum (using the ERC-721 or ERC-1155 standards), though other blockchains like Solana, Flow, and Polygon also host large NFT ecosystems.

History & Boom

While early NFT experiments like CryptoPunks (2017) predate the mainstream NFT boom, it was 2021 that saw explosive growth. Digital artist Beeple sold an NFT artwork for $69 million at Christie's auction house in March 2021, triggering mainstream media coverage. Collections like Bored Ape Yacht Club (BAYC) became cultural phenomena with floor prices reaching hundreds of ETH.

Use Cases

  • Digital Art — Artist royalties, provenance tracking
  • Gaming — In-game items with real ownership (Axie Infinity, Gods Unchained)
  • Music — Artists sell directly to fans, embed royalties
  • Collectibles — Sports moments (NBA Top Shot), trading cards
  • Identity & Membership — Token-gated communities and events
  • Real-World Assets — Tokenised property, luxury goods

Community Views

NFTs remain highly polarising. Supporters view them as a revolutionary tool for creator monetisation and digital ownership. Sceptics argue most are speculative assets with no inherent value. The crypto community is divided, with many "NFT degens" defending the space and others dismissing it entirely.

"NFTs aren't just JPEGs — they're programmable ownership on the internet." — Common pro-NFT argument on Twitter/X[1]
