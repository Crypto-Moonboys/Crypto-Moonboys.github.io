DeFi (Decentralised Finance)

✅ Verified📅 Last updated: 2024-01📂 Concepts
📋 Contents

DeFi — Decentralised Finance — is banking without banks. It's lending, borrowing, trading, and earning yield without filling out a form, showing ID, or waiting three business days for a wire to clear. DeFi runs on Ethereum smart contracts 24/7/365, and in just a few years it grew from absolute zero to $200 billion+ in total value locked. This isn't the future of finance. This is the financial system of the future, live right now.

History & Origins

Early Foundations (2017–2019)

The DeFi story begins on the blockchain before most people had even heard the word. MakerDAO launched in December 2017 as the first true DeFi protocol, introducing DAI — a crypto-collateralised stablecoin soft-pegged to the US dollar. Then came Compound in September 2018, bringing algorithmic money markets, and Uniswap v1 in November 2018, created by Hayden Adams in his apartment after being laid off — inventing the automated market maker (AMM) model that still dominates DEX trading today.

DeFi Summer 2020 🔥

Nothing in crypto history compares to DeFi Summer. In June 2020, Compound launched its COMP governance token via liquidity mining, sparking an era of yield-chasing that went parabolic. Within months:

  • Uniswap v2 (May 2020) hit $1B+ volume in its first month
  • Curve Finance (Jan 2020) became the go-to stablecoin DEX
  • Yearn Finance launched July 2020 by Andre Cronje — yield aggregation for degens
  • SushiSwap (Aug 2020) executed the legendary "vampire attack" — forked Uniswap's code, launched a SUSHI token, and drained $1B of liquidity in 72 hours
  • Total DeFi TVL hit $1B in September 2020, then $10B by November 2020

The Peak & the Crash (2021–2022)

DeFi TVL peaked at approximately $180 billion in November 2021 (per DefiLlama). Then the dominoes fell. The Terra/Luna UST collapse in May 2022 wiped $50B overnight and exposed the fatal flaw of algorithmic stablecoins. Celsius, Three Arrows Capital, and BlockFi followed — but critically, these were all centralised entities. DeFi protocols kept running. The smart contracts didn't go bankrupt. The narrative was proven.

Rebirth & Real Yield (2023–2025)

Post-crash DeFi rebuilt smarter. Real yield protocols, restaking via EigenLayer (launched Aug 2023, $20B+ TVL by 2024), points programs, and sophisticated new primitives emerged. Uniswap v4, Pendle yield-trading, and Ethena's synthetic dollar USDe pushed the design space forward. DeFi didn't die — it graduated.

How It Works / Key Concepts

Automated Market Makers (AMMs)

Forget order books. AMMs use liquidity pools — pairs of tokens locked in smart contracts — priced by the constant-product formula x × y = k. Anyone can become a liquidity provider (LP) by depositing token pairs and earning a share of trading fees. Uniswap, Curve, and Balancer each implement AMM variants optimised for different use cases.

Lending & Borrowing

Protocols like Aave and Compound enable over-collateralised borrowing — deposit ETH, borrow USDC. Interest rates adjust algorithmically based on utilisation rates. If your collateral drops below the liquidation threshold, bots liquidate you instantly. No mercy, no appeals process.

Flash Loans

The most mind-bending DeFi primitive: uncollateralised loans that must be repaid within a single transaction block. If you can't repay, the whole transaction reverts as if it never happened. Used for arbitrage, liquidations, and — infamously — exploits. Aave v2 (Dec 2020) made flash loans mainstream.

Yield Farming & Liquidity Mining

Protocols emit governance tokens to users who provide liquidity — aligning incentives and bootstrapping usage. At peak DeFi Summer, 4-digit APYs were common (if unsustainable). Mercenary capital would farm a protocol, dump the token, and move on. Real yield protocols shifted to sharing actual fee revenue instead of pure emissions.

Oracles & Stablecoins

Smart contracts can't access off-chain data natively. Chainlink oracles provide price feeds that DeFi protocols depend on. Stablecoins — USDC (centralised), DAI (decentralised), USDT (centralised but dominant) — are the lifeblood of DeFi, providing a stable unit of account across the ecosystem.

Cross-Chain Bridges

As DeFi expanded to Arbitrum, Optimism, Base, Avalanche, and Solana, bridges became critical infrastructure — and the most exploited infrastructure in crypto. Moving assets between chains requires trusting bridge smart contracts, which have proven catastrophically vulnerable.

Why It Matters

1.4 billion people globally are unbanked. DeFi requires nothing but an internet connection and a crypto wallet — no passport, no credit score, no bank account. Markets are open 24/7/365. Yield on idle assets is available to anyone. Centralised exchanges can freeze your funds; DeFi smart contracts cannot. Web3's financial layer is DeFi, and Ethereum is its home.

DeFi is also the most transparent financial system ever created. Every transaction, every liquidity pool, every loan is visible on-chain. No hidden leverage, no fractional reserve tricks — just code and math.

Risks & Controversies

⚠️
High Risk: DeFi protocols have lost billions to exploits, rug pulls, and economic attacks. This is not a space for money you cannot afford to lose.
  • Smart contract exploits — Over $5B hacked 2020–2024. Code is law, but buggy code is a catastrophe.
  • Bridge hacks — Ronin Network ($625M, March 2022), Wormhole ($320M, Feb 2022), Nomad ($190M, Aug 2022). Bridges are the #1 attack surface in crypto.
  • Rug pulls — Anonymous devs drain liquidity pools overnight. See Rug Pull Wars.
  • Impermanent loss — LPs can underperform simply holding when asset prices diverge significantly.
  • Oracle manipulation — Flash loan attacks exploit price feed delays to drain lending protocols.
  • Whale manipulation — Large actors can move markets and governance votes. See Whale Lords.
  • Regulatory pressure — CFTC action against dYdX, SEC targeting Uniswap Labs — regulators are circling. Permissionless doesn't mean consequence-free.
