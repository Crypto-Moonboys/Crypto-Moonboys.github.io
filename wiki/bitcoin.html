Bitcoin (BTC)

⭐ Featured 📅 Last updated: 2024-01 📂 Cryptocurrencies
📋 Contents

Bitcoin (BTC) is the OG — the alpha, the genesis block of the entire crypto universe. Born from the smouldering wreckage of the 2008 global financial crisis, it is the hardest money ever created, running on pure mathematics and cryptographic proof. No CEO. No board of directors. No bailout. Just 21 million coins, an immutable ledger, and the most battle-tested decentralised network on planet Earth. Everything else in crypto stands on the shoulders of this giant.

History & Origins

The Whitepaper (2008)

On 31 October 2008 — Halloween — an anonymous cryptographer (or group) operating under the name Satoshi Nakamoto dropped a nine-page PDF onto a cryptography mailing list. Titled "Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System," it solved a problem computer scientists had wrestled with for decades: how do you prevent someone from spending the same digital money twice without a trusted middleman? The answer was the blockchain — a distributed, cryptographically linked chain of transaction records maintained by thousands of independent nodes.

The Genesis Block (2009)

The Bitcoin network went live on 3 January 2009, when Satoshi mined Block 0 — the genesis block. Embedded inside that first block was a message that became the founding manifesto of the movement:

"The Times 03/Jan/2009 Chancellor on brink of second bailout for banks."

That headline — from the front page of a British newspaper — was Satoshi's timestamp and a middle finger to the existing financial order. While central banks were printing money to bail out the institutions that caused the crisis, Satoshi was bootstrapping a monetary system with a fixed supply and rules set in code, not politics.

On 12 January 2009, early cryptography legend Hal Finney received the first-ever Bitcoin transaction: 10 BTC from Satoshi himself. Finney had been one of the few people who immediately grasped what Bitcoin could become.

Bitcoin Pizza Day (2010)

On 22 May 2010, Florida programmer Laszlo Hanyecz paid 10,000 BTC for two Papa John's pizzas — the first documented real-world Bitcoin purchase. At the time, those coins were worth roughly $41. At Bitcoin's 2025 all-time high, that pizza bill hit over $1 billion. Laszlo has no regrets (he says). Every 22 May, the crypto world celebrates Bitcoin Pizza Day — a reminder that early adoption means eating the loss of buying pizza with future billions.

Mt. Gox & the Wild West Era (2010–2014)

The early exchange Mt. Gox, originally a Magic: The Gathering card trading site repurposed into a Bitcoin exchange, handled over 70% of all Bitcoin transactions at its peak. In February 2014, it collapsed spectacularly, revealing that approximately 850,000 BTC (then ~$460 million) had been stolen over several years. The hack devastated confidence and sent Bitcoin from ~$800 to below $300. It remains the largest crypto exchange hack in history in terms of nominal BTC. The Mt. Gox saga is Exhibit A for why self-custody and reputable exchanges matter.

First $1,000 & Cyprus (2013)

In late 2013, Bitcoin crossed $1,000 for the first time, supercharged in part by the Cyprus banking crisis — where the EU imposed a deposit levy on savers. Ordinary people watching their bank accounts get confiscated suddenly found Bitcoin's "not your keys, not your coins" mantra very compelling. Bitcoin briefly hit $1,242 before crashing hard in 2014.

The 2017 Bull Run & $20,000

The ICO boom of 2017 propelled Bitcoin to a then-ATH of $19,783 on 17 December 2017. Retail FOMO was at an all-time high. Your dentist, your uncle, and your Uber driver all wanted Bitcoin. The CME Bitcoin Futures launched in December 2017, the first regulated derivative product, marking a new era of institutional involvement — and, some argue, immediate short-selling that helped pop the bubble.

Institutional Wave (2020–2021)

The 2020–2021 cycle was different. This time, suits were buying. MicroStrategy's CEO Michael Saylor made the first major corporate Bitcoin bet in August 2020, calling BTC "digital gold" and a superior treasury reserve asset. MicroStrategy has continued buying relentlessly and now holds over 500,000 BTC — the largest corporate Bitcoin treasury on earth. Tesla added $1.5B in early 2021. Square (now Block) under Jack Dorsey bought $50M. Hedge funds, family offices, and sovereign wealth vehicles all began allocating.

Bitcoin hit a new ATH of $69,044 on 10 November 2021, with a market cap briefly topping $1.3 trillion.

FTX Collapse & the Crypto Winter (2022)

November 2022 was brutal. The collapse of FTX — once the world's third-largest crypto exchange, run by Sam Bankman-Fried — sent shockwaves through the entire market. An estimated $8B+ in customer funds had been misappropriated. Bitcoin plunged to roughly $15,500 — a 78% drawdown from the ATH. Trust in centralised exchanges cratered. Ironically, the fraud vindicated Bitcoin's core message: don't trust institutions, trust math. See Crypto Exchanges for how to evaluate exchange safety.

The ETF Era & Six Figures (2023–2025)

The recovery was historic. In 2023, BlackRock — the world's largest asset manager with $10 trillion AUM — filed for a spot Bitcoin ETF. When BlackRock files, people pay attention. On 10 January 2024, the US Securities and Exchange Commission approved the first batch of spot Bitcoin ETFs, including products from BlackRock (iShares), Fidelity, Invesco, and others. Within weeks, these ETFs had absorbed over $50 billion in assets — the fastest ETF launch in history.

The 4th Bitcoin Halving occurred on 19–20 April 2024, reducing the block reward from 6.25 BTC to 3.125 BTC. History suggests halvings are rocket fuel for the four-year cycle.

Bitcoin crossed $100,000 for the first time in December 2024, then set a new all-time high above $109,000 in January 2025. And in March 2025, the United States announced a Strategic Bitcoin Reserve — the US government officially treating Bitcoin as a national strategic asset. If you told a Bitcoiner in 2013 this would happen, they'd have thought you were delusional. Here we are.

How It Works

Blockchain Technology

At its core, Bitcoin is a distributed ledger — a database replicated across tens of thousands of nodes worldwide. Every transaction ever made is recorded on the blockchain, and every node independently verifies the full history. There is no central server to hack, no database to corrupt, no company to shut down. The blockchain is maintained by consensus — the majority of the network must agree on valid transactions.

Proof of Work Mining

Proof of Work (PoW) is the consensus mechanism that secures Bitcoin. Miners — specialised computers (ASICs) — race to solve a cryptographic puzzle: find a number (nonce) that, when combined with block data and hashed with SHA-256, produces an output with a required number of leading zeroes. The difficulty adjusts every 2,016 blocks (~2 weeks) to ensure blocks are found approximately every 10 minutes, regardless of how much hashing power joins or leaves the network.

The winner gets to add the next block and earns the block reward (currently 3.125 BTC) plus all transaction fees in that block. This is how new Bitcoin is created — through provable computational work, not by decree. The total network hashrate has grown to 700+ exahashes per second by 2025, making a 51% attack economically impossible for any realistic attacker.

The Halving Schedule

Every 210,000 blocks (roughly every four years), the block reward is cut in half. This built-in supply reduction — the halving — is Bitcoin's monetary policy, coded at birth and unchangeable. The halvings have correlated strongly with each subsequent bull market:

Wallets & Private Keys

Bitcoin ownership is controlled through cryptographic key pairs. Your private key — a 256-bit secret number — is used to sign transactions authorising the movement of funds. The corresponding public key (and derived address) is what others send Bitcoin to. If you lose your private key, your Bitcoin is gone forever. If someone else gets your private key, they own your Bitcoin. The community mantra: "Not your keys, not your coins." See Crypto Wallets for a deep dive on hardware wallets, seed phrases, and self-custody best practices.

Lightning Network

The base layer Bitcoin blockchain processes roughly 7 transactions per second (TPS) — intentionally limited to maintain decentralisation. For everyday payments, the Lightning Network (a Layer 2 payment channel protocol) enables instant, near-free Bitcoin transactions. Lightning routes payments through a network of bidirectional payment channels, settling only the final state on-chain. El Salvador uses Lightning for its national Bitcoin wallet (Chivo), and services like Strike enable Lightning payments at the point of sale globally.

The UTXO Model

Bitcoin doesn't track account balances like a bank. Instead, it uses the Unspent Transaction Output (UTXO) model. Your "balance" is really the sum of all unspent outputs (chunks of BTC) that have been sent to your address and haven't been spent yet. Every transaction consumes UTXOs as inputs and creates new UTXOs as outputs. This design enhances privacy and enables parallel transaction validation.

Why It Matters

Bitcoin isn't just an investment — it's an entirely new monetary paradigm. Here's why it matters beyond the price chart:

  • Digital Gold & Store of Value: With a hard cap of 21 million coins and a decreasing issuance rate, Bitcoin is fundamentally scarce. No government, no corporation, no algorithm can inflate away your holdings. This makes it a compelling hedge against monetary debasement — something particularly relevant in a world of trillion-dollar fiscal deficits.
  • Censorship Resistance: No bank can freeze your Bitcoin. No government can seize it without your keys. For billions of people living under authoritarian regimes or hyperinflation (Venezuela, Argentina, Nigeria), Bitcoin is a literal financial lifeline.
  • Institutional Adoption: The spot Bitcoin ETF approval in January 2024 brought Bitcoin into the portfolios of pension funds, endowments, and retail investors through traditional brokerage accounts. BlackRock's IBIT became one of the fastest ETFs to $10B in AUM ever. The genie is out of the bottle.
  • US Strategic Reserve: The March 2025 announcement of a US Strategic Bitcoin Reserve — treating Bitcoin alongside gold as a national reserve asset — is the most significant political validation in Bitcoin's history.
  • El Salvador & Sovereign Adoption: El Salvador became the first country to adopt Bitcoin as legal tender in September 2021, with President Nayib Bukele purchasing BTC for the national treasury. Despite IMF objections, El Salvador's Bitcoin bet has paid off handsomely.
  • Foundation for Crypto: Bitcoin's blockchain technology spawned an entire ecosystem of innovation — from DeFi to Web3 — though Bitcoin itself remains deliberately conservative in its evolution, prioritising security over features.

Risks & Controversies

No asset with revolutionary potential comes without real risks. Be informed:

  • Energy Consumption: Bitcoin mining consumes approximately 120–150 TWh per year — comparable to a mid-size country. Critics argue this is wasteful; proponents counter that an increasing share comes from renewable energy, that miners use otherwise-curtailed or stranded energy, and that the energy is purchasing global financial sovereignty.
  • 51% Attack (Theoretical): If a single entity controlled over 50% of the network's hashrate, it could theoretically double-spend or rewrite recent transaction history. With 700+ EH/s of hashrate, this would require billions in hardware and energy — making it economically irrational. Still, miner pool concentration bears watching.
  • Quantum Computing Threat: Sufficiently powerful quantum computers could theoretically break Bitcoin's elliptic curve cryptography. However, this threat is decades away (if ever practical), and the Bitcoin protocol can be upgraded to quantum-resistant cryptography if needed.
  • Regulatory Risk: Governments could impose heavy restrictions on Bitcoin ownership, mining, or exchange. China has "banned" Bitcoin multiple times. The US, EU, and others continue to develop frameworks. Regulatory clarity is improving but remains a risk.
  • Satoshi's Identity: We don't know who Satoshi Nakamoto is. This is both a feature (no single point of failure, regulatory pressure target) and a concern (what if they still hold ~1M BTC and sell?). Satoshi's wallets haven't moved in over a decade.
  • Lost Coins: An estimated 3–4 million BTC are permanently inaccessible — lost keys, forgotten wallets, intentional burns. This reduces the effective supply but also highlights the importance of proper key management.
