Bitcoin (BTC)

⭐ Featured 📅 Last updated: 2024-01 📂 Cryptocurrencies
📋 Contents

Bitcoin (BTC) is the world's first decentralised digital currency and the largest cryptocurrency by market capitalisation. It was created in 2009 by an anonymous individual or group using the pseudonym Satoshi Nakamoto, who published a whitepaper titled "Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System". Bitcoin operates without a central authority, using blockchain technology to enable trustless, permissionless transactions.

History

Origins (2008–2009)

On 31 October 2008, in the aftermath of the global financial crisis, Satoshi Nakamoto published the Bitcoin whitepaper on a cryptography mailing list. The paper proposed a system for electronic transactions that did not require trust in a third party — addressing the double-spending problem using a chain of cryptographic proofs.

The Bitcoin network officially launched on 3 January 2009, when Nakamoto mined the first block — known as the genesis block — containing the now-famous embedded message:

"The Times 03/Jan/2009 Chancellor on brink of second bailout for banks."

This embedded headline, referencing a UK newspaper front page, is widely interpreted as a commentary on the instability of the traditional banking system that Bitcoin aimed to replace.

Early Adoption (2010–2013)

On 22 May 2010, programmer Laszlo Hanyecz made the first documented real-world Bitcoin transaction, purchasing two pizzas for 10,000 BTC — now celebrated annually as Bitcoin Pizza Day. The date marks the moment Bitcoin gained real monetary value.

In 2011, Bitcoin reached parity with the US dollar for the first time and later peaked above $30, before crashing back. This early volatility became a hallmark of the asset. The first major exchanges — including Mt. Gox — emerged in this period.

Mainstream Awareness (2017–2021)

Bitcoin's 2017 bull run brought it to global mainstream attention, with prices briefly surpassing $20,000. The subsequent crash and recovery cycle repeated in 2020–2021, when Bitcoin reached an all-time high above $69,000 in November 2021, driven by institutional adoption from companies like MicroStrategy, Tesla, and Square.

How It Works

Blockchain

Bitcoin transactions are recorded on a public, distributed ledger called the blockchain. Each block contains a batch of verified transactions, a timestamp, and a cryptographic hash of the previous block — linking them in a chronological chain. See Blockchain Technology for a deeper explanation.

Proof of Work (Mining)

Bitcoin uses a Proof of Work (PoW) consensus mechanism. Miners — nodes on the network — compete to solve a computationally intensive mathematical puzzle. The first to solve it adds the next block and earns a block reward of newly minted Bitcoin plus transaction fees. This process secures the network and controls the issuance of new coins.

The Halving

Every 210,000 blocks (approximately every 4 years), the block reward is cut in half — an event known as the halving. This built-in supply reduction is central to Bitcoin's deflationary monetary policy. The halvings have historically correlated with significant bull markets as supply issuance decreases while demand grows.

Wallets & Keys

Bitcoin is controlled through cryptographic key pairs. A private key is used to sign transactions, while the corresponding public key (or address) is what others use to send you Bitcoin. "Not your keys, not your coins" is a popular motto in the community, emphasising self-custody. See Crypto Wallets.

Supply & Economics

Bitcoin has a hard-capped maximum supply of 21 million BTC, making it fundamentally scarce — a property often compared to gold. As of 2024, approximately 19.7 million BTC have been mined. The remaining supply will be released gradually until around the year 2140.

Bitcoin is commonly referred to as "digital gold" due to its scarcity, store-of-value properties, and resistance to censorship and inflation.

Community Views

Bitcoin has one of the most passionate communities in crypto. Bitcoin maximalists (or "maxis") believe Bitcoin is the only true cryptocurrency, while a broader segment of the crypto community views it as just one asset among many.

"Bitcoin is not just a currency. It's a protest against the existing financial system." — Common sentiment on Crypto Twitter/X[1]

Popular crypto YouTube channels such as BitBoy Crypto, Coin Bureau, and Anthony Pompliano's channel have published extensively on Bitcoin's potential as a reserve asset, often citing macro-economic concerns around central bank money printing as a bullish catalyst.[2]

Cultural Impact

Bitcoin has spawned an entire cultural movement. Terms like HODL (hold), to the moon, diamond hands, and FUD (Fear, Uncertainty, Doubt) originated in or were popularised by the Bitcoin and broader crypto community. The Moonboys term itself — a community archetype known for extreme optimism — has deep roots in Bitcoin culture.

Criticisms & Concerns

  • Energy consumption: Bitcoin mining requires significant electricity, drawing criticism from environmentalists. Proponents argue increasing renewable energy use mitigates this.
  • Volatility: Bitcoin's price can swing dramatically in short periods, raising questions about its suitability as a medium of exchange.
  • Scalability: The base layer processes ~7 transactions per second (TPS). Solutions like the Lightning Network aim to address this for micropayments.
  • Regulatory risk: Governments worldwide have taken varying stances, from outright bans to official legal tender status (El Salvador, 2021).
